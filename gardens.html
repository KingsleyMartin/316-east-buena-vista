Pioneering High-Desert Horticulture

Innovative Design Principles

Charlotte Wilson's gardens were considered among the finest in Santa Fe during the 1920s-1940s, pioneering sustainable water management in the high desert. Her terraced design maximized water efficiency while creating distinct microclimates for different plant varieties.

Sluiceway Irrigation System

The innovative sluiceway irrigation system directed water from the property's pre-basin well through carefully designed channels, allowing Charlotte to control water flow to different garden areas based on seasonal needs and plant requirements.

Cross Orchard Connection

The property's well originally served the Cross Orchard, one of Santa Fe's significant agricultural operations. Charlotte adapted this agricultural infrastructure for sophisticated residential landscaping.

Terraced Garden Layout

Multiple terraced levels created optimal growing conditions for diverse plantings while managing water runoff and erosion control - essential in Santa Fe's challenging climate.

Plant Selection Strategy

  • Fruit trees adapted to high altitude and temperature extremes
  • Vegetable gardens in protected, irrigated areas
  • Native plants for drought tolerance and wildlife habitat
  • Shade gardens in courtyard and portal areas
  • Cutting gardens for indoor flower arrangements

Social Significance

The Wilson gardens served as a venue for Santa Fe's cultural community, hosting garden parties and serving as a model for other high-desert landscaping projects throughout the region.

Garden Evolution Through Time

125+ years of landscape development and horticultural innovation

1900s

Cross Orchard Era

Property served as part of larger agricultural operation with hand-dug well and basic irrigation infrastructure. Original fruit trees planted, establishing the foundation for future landscaping efforts.

1920s

Charlotte Wilson's Design Peak

Sophisticated terraced garden design implemented with innovative sluiceway irrigation. Diverse plantings including fruit trees, vegetables, cutting gardens, and native plants. Gardens gained regional recognition for beauty and water efficiency.

1950s

Transition & Adaptation

Following Charlotte Wilson's death (1954), gardens maintained by subsequent owners but with reduced complexity. Core fruit trees and infrastructure preserved while ornamental plantings simplified.

1980s

Rental Period Decline

During rental property years, gardens experienced significant neglect. Irrigation systems fell into disrepair, ornamental plantings were lost, though mature fruit trees survived with minimal care.

1995

Kent-Love Revival

David Kent & Margaret Love restored basic garden functionality, rediscovered historic well system producing 15-20 GPM, and installed modern irrigation controllers while respecting historic layout principles.

2025

Comprehensive Restoration

Current restoration project aims to recreate Charlotte Wilson's terraced design using sustainable modern techniques while preserving historic plant materials and water-efficient irrigation principles.

Pre-Basin Well: Historic Water Rights & Documentation

Legal Status & Significance

The property's well is classified as a pre-basin well, meaning it was established before the State Engineer declared the Rio Grande Basin on November 29, 1956. This designation is legally significant as it grants water rights without requiring a diversion permit from the State Engineer.

The well potentially dates to pre-1907, which would give the associated water rights special legal status under New Mexico water law. Physical evidence supports this early date, including the well house constructed of structural clay tile brick likely manufactured no later than the mid-1940s.

Historical Context

The well was likely hand-dug in the late 1800s or early 1900s to serve the Cross Orchard, a significant agricultural operation that originally encompassed this property. At depths of 15-25 feet, water would have been encountered in this location.

The property's location above the Acequia Madre lateral made gravity-fed irrigation from the acequia impossible, necessitating well water for irrigation. This explains the early development of well infrastructure when pumps were not yet commonâ€”the hand-dug well provided the essential water source for orchard cultivation.

Modern Rediscovery & Restoration

When David Kent and Margaret Love purchased the property in the mid-1990s, the garden had been neglected for years and was overgrown with elm trees. They discovered a two-inch irrigation pipe along the fence line leading to a rusty pump.

Upon investigation, they found the well itself had survived the period of neglect. After clearing debris and testing, the well produced an impressive 15-20 gallons per minuteâ€”more than sufficient for comprehensive landscape irrigation. They installed new two-inch polypropylene feeder lines four feet underground with irrigation controllers in the well house.

Documentation Through Time

  • 1866 map: Shows Acequia Madre on property's north side
  • 1912 King's Map: Documents well and windmill location
  • 1914 State Engineer survey: Notes hydrant irrigation on property
  • 1955 survey: Shows "pump house" at current well location
  • 1977 State Engineer survey: Property documented under Tract 11.145 and 11.144
  • 1995 restoration: Kent-Love rediscovery and system modernization

The Federici family, who owned the property from the 1950s to about 1980, confirmed the well was present and operational for landscape irrigation throughout their ownership.

Charlotte Wilson's Integration of Well and Sluiceway

Charlotte Wilson's innovative garden design ingeniously combined the property's pre-existing well with her sophisticated sluiceway irrigation system. As documented in 1930s memoirs, "The sloping land was terraced and irrigated by a unique system of sluiceways which took water from one level to the next." This gravity-fed distribution system worked in harmony with the well's steady 15-20 GPM flow, allowing precise control of water delivery to different garden zones while maximizing efficiency in Santa Fe's challenging high-desert climate.
