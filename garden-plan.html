1Executive Summary

This irrigation system design provides comprehensive coverage for 24,209 square feet across 12 distinct zones at the historic 316 East Buena Vista Street property. The system is engineered to fully utilize the 3 acre-foot annual water allocation (977,553 gallons), supporting lush, vibrant estate gardens appropriate for this significant Santa Fe landmark.

The design incorporates a hybrid approach: efficient drip irrigation for trees, shrubs, roses, and vegetables, combined with spray systems for lawn and ground cover areas requiring uniform coverage. A smart controller with weather-based ET adjustment optimizes watering schedules while a cistern-based pressurization system ensures reliable water delivery from the existing well.

Total projected annual water usage is 950,000 gallons (97% of allocation), maintaining a small buffer while maximizing the property's horticultural potential.

5Main Line Configuration

The irrigation system utilizes a pressurized supply from the cistern system, which receives water from the well house. The main line configuration consists of:

  • Main Line 1 (West): 1.25" Schedule 40 PVC, 279 linear feet, running from the cistern/pump house along the western property boundary, serving zones Z1, Z2, Z3, Z4, Z5, and Z6.
  • Branch Lines (6 total): 1" Schedule 40 PVC laterals extending from Main Line 1:
    • Branch 1: 22 ft to Z7 (Trees & Grasses NE)
    • Branch 2: 17 ft to Z8 (Lawn)
    • Branch 3: 17 ft to Z9 (Shrubs & Bushes)
    • Branch 4: 20 ft to Z10 (Ground Cover)
    • Branch 5: 41 ft to Z10 (Trees & Plantings)
    • Branch 6: 15 ft to Z11 (Pond Area)
  • Total Line Length: 411 feet of main and branch lines
  • Pressure Source: 1-2 HP pump from dual cisterns (10,000 gallon total capacity) providing 45-55 PSI system pressure
  • Valve Manifolds: Two manifold locations for simplified maintenance—one near the cistern (6 valves) and one at the northeast garden area (6 valves)

9Installation Notes

Key considerations for this historic Santa Fe property:

  • Historic Adobe Protection: All trenching must maintain minimum 3-foot clearance from adobe foundation walls. Use hand digging within 6 feet of structures to prevent vibration damage. Coordinate with any historic preservation requirements.
  • High Desert Climate Adaptation: At 7,000' elevation, UV exposure degrades exposed materials quickly. Use UV-stabilized drip tubing and bury main lines minimum 12" deep to prevent freeze damage. All above-ground components must be UV-rated.
  • Freeze Protection: Install manual drain valves at all low points in the system. Main lines and branch lines must slope toward drain points. Backflow preventer requires insulated cover for winter. System winterization typically required by mid-October.
  • Stand Pipes for Winter Tree Watering: Eight stand pipes with quick-connect fittings distributed throughout tree zones allow manual deep watering during winter months when the main system is winterized. Each stand pipe connects to the pressurized cistern supply independently.
  • Cistern System Integration: Dual 5,000-gallon cisterns provide 10,000 gallons total storage capacity. Coordinate pump sizing with well recovery rate. Install low-water cutoff switch to protect pump. Float valves on cistern fills must be calibrated to maintain adequate reserve for irrigation cycles. Plumb cisterns in parallel for balanced draw-down.
  • Soil Considerations: Santa Fe's adobe clay soils have low infiltration rates. Use cycle-soak watering for spray zones (multiple short cycles vs. one long cycle) to prevent runoff. Drip systems naturally accommodate slow infiltration.
  • Charlotte Wilson Garden Heritage: The historic terraced garden design should be preserved. Route irrigation lines along existing bed edges and pathways. Avoid disturbing established heritage fruit trees—use surface drip rings rather than trenching near root zones.
  • Electrical Requirements: Pump requires dedicated 240V/20A circuit. Smart controller needs 120V outlet in protected location. Consider adding GFCI-protected outdoor outlets near pond pump location.

10Maintenance Schedule

🌸 Spring (March - May)

  • System startup: Check all valves, flush lines
  • Inspect and clean drip emitters
  • Replace damaged spray heads
  • Verify controller programming and sensor function
  • Test rain sensor and soil moisture sensors
  • Check pump pressure and cistern fill rate
  • Adjust spray head patterns for new growth
  • Apply pre-emergent to prevent weeds in drip zones

☀️ Summer (June - August)

  • Weekly visual inspection of all zones
  • Monitor water usage via flow meter
  • Adjust controller for monsoon season (July-Aug)
  • Check for clogged emitters monthly
  • Inspect drip tubing for UV damage or leaks
  • Verify even coverage in spray zones
  • Clean filters monthly during peak use
  • Monitor cistern levels during high-demand periods

🍂 Fall (September - November)

  • Gradually reduce watering frequency
  • Deep water trees before winterization
  • Clean and inspect all filters
  • Winterize system before hard freeze (mid-October)
  • Blow out lines with compressed air
  • Drain backflow preventer, install cover
  • Disconnect and store hoses
  • Verify stand pipes operational for winter use

❄️ Winter (December - February)

  • Monthly tree watering via stand pipes
  • Water trees 15-20 gallons each when ground unfrozen
  • Focus on evergreens and newly planted trees
  • Check insulation on backflow preventer
  • Inspect pump house for freeze protection
  • Plan any system modifications for spring
  • Order replacement parts as needed
  • Review water usage records from prior year
Winter Tree Watering Protocol 26 trees require supplemental watering monthly during dormant season. Using stand pipes with quick-connects, apply 20 gallons per tree (520 gallons total) on a day when soil is unfrozen and temperatures are above 40°F. Focus especially on evergreens and any trees planted within the last 3 years.
